Explanation

The Principle of Stationary Action

Classical mechanics can be reformulated not as a story of forces and accelerations, but as a problem of optimization. Instead of asking what forces act on a particle, we ask: what path must a particle take through its configuration space to minimize a quantity called the action? This is the core idea behind the Principle of Stationary Action, also known as Hamilton's Principle. It provides a more general and powerful framework than Newtonian mechanics, especially for complex systems.

The action, denoted by SSS, is defined as the time integral of the Lagrangian, L=TVL = T - VL=TV, where TTT is the kinetic energy and VVV is the potential energy of the system.

S[q(t)]=t1t2L(q,q,t)dtS[q(t)] = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(q, \dot{q}, t) \, dtS[q(t)]=t1t2L(q,q,t)dt

Hamilton's Principle states that the actual path taken by a system between two points in time is the one that makes the action stationary (a minimum, maximum, or saddle point).

To find this stationary path, we use the calculus of variations. We consider a small perturbation, q(t)\delta q(t)q(t), to the true path q(t)q(t)q(t). The condition for a stationary point is that the first variation of the action, S\delta SS, must be zero for any small perturbation that vanishes at the endpoints (q(t1)=q(t2)=0\delta q(t_1) = \delta q(t_2) = 0q(t1)=q(t2)=0).

S=t1t2(Lqq+Lqq)dt=0\delta S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial q} \delta q + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}} \delta \dot{q} \right) dt = 0S=t1t2(qLq+qLq)dt=0

Using integration by parts on the second term, and remembering that q=ddt(q)\delta \dot{q} = \frac{d}{dt}(\delta q)q=dtd(q), we can rewrite the expression. The boundary terms disappear because q\delta qq is zero at the endpoints. This manipulation leads directly to a profound result.

t1t2(LqddtLq)qdt=0\int_{t_1} ^{t_2} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial q} - \frac{d}{dt} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}} \right) \delta q \, dt = 0t1t2(qLdtdqL)qdt=0

The result is the Euler-Lagrange equation, the fundamental equation of motion in Lagrangian mechanics.

LqiddtLqi=0\frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i} - \frac{d}{dt} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i} = 0qiLdtdqiL=0

Generalized Coordinates and Constraints

The true power of the Lagrangian formulation becomes apparent when dealing with constrained systems. Newtonian mechanics requires explicitly solving for constraint forces, which can be cumbersome. Lagrangian mechanics bypasses this by using generalized coordinates.

Generalized coordinates are a set of independent variables that completely specify the state of the system while automatically satisfying the constraints. For example, a pendulum bob moving in a plane has two Cartesian coordinates (xxx, yyy) but is constrained by x2+y2=l2x^2 + y^2 = l^2x2+y2=l2. We can instead use a single generalized coordinate, the angle \theta, which respects the constraint implicitly. The configuration space is no longer flat Euclidean space, but a curved manifold.

"So the way to solve practically any problem in classical mechanics is to express its Lagrangian in terms of the generalized coordinates and their derivatives, use the Euler-Lagrange equations to get the differential equation(s) for the system, then solve the differential equations for the given boundary conditions."

Constraints are classified based on their mathematical form.

holonomic
A constraint that can be expressed as an algebraic equation relating the coordinates of the system, $$f(q_1, q_2, ..., q_n, t) = 0$$. These constraints reduce the number of degrees of freedom.

Non- holonomic constraints cannot be written in this way; they typically involve inequalities or relationships between velocities that cannot be integrated to a form relating only coordinates. A classic example is a sphere rolling without slipping on a plane. The constraint relates its linear and angular velocities, but you cannot integrate this to find a simple relationship between its position and orientation.

d'Alembert's Principle

Before Hamilton's principle provided a complete variational framework, d'Alembert's principle offered a bridge from Newtonian mechanics to the methods of analytical mechanics. It's essentially a differential principle, a statement about the state of a system at a single instant in time.

The principle states that for any virtual displacement ri\delta \mathbf{r}_iri (an infinitesimal displacement consistent with the constraints at a fixed instant of time), the total virtual work done by the constraint forces is zero. This allows us to ignore the forces of constraint when writing the equations of motion.

i(Fipi)ri=0\sum_i (\mathbf{F}_i - \dot{\mathbf{p}}_i) \cdot \delta \mathbf{r}_i = 0i(Fipi)ri=0

By expressing the virtual displacements ri\delta \mathbf{r}_iri in terms of generalized coordinates qjq_jqj, d'Alembert's principle can be used to derive the Euler-Lagrange equations. While both methods lead to the same result for holonomic systems, Hamilton's Principle is an integral principle that considers the entire path, making it a more fundamental and elegant statement about the nature of physical laws.
