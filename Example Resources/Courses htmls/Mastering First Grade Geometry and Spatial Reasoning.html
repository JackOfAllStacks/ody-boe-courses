Defining Attributes

Explanation

The Rules of Shapes

We know a triangle when we see one. But why is it a triangle? Is it because it's blue? Or because it's small? Or because it's pointing up?

None of those things matter. A triangle can be any color, any size, and point in any direction. Those are its non-defining attributes. They can change without changing what the shape is.

The important stuffthe defining attributes are the rules a shape must follow. For a triangle, the rules are simple: it must have exactly three straight sides and three corners.

Think of it like a recipe. The ingredients and steps are the defining attributes. If you change them, you get a different dish. The color of the plate you serve it on is a non-defining attribute. It doesn't change the food itself.

Every geometric shape has its own set of rules. These rules are what allow us to classify them.

Sides and Vertices

Two of the most important rules for many shapes involve their sides and vertices.

  • Sides are the straight line segments that form a shape.
  • Vertices are the corners where the sides meet.

A shape with three sides must also have three vertices. A shape with four sides has four vertices. This pattern holds true for all polygons.

Vertex
A point where two or more lines or edges meet; a corner.

These features are non-negotiable. If you add a side to a square, it stops being a square and becomes a pentagon. The number of sides and vertices defines the shape.

Closed vs. Open

Another crucial defining attribute is whether a figure is closed or open. A shape is closed if all its lines connect, leaving no gaps. You can trace its path and end up where you started.

Polygons, such as triangles, squares, and pentagons, must be closed figures. If there's any opening, it's not a polygon. It's just a collection of connected lines.

This rule helps us create a clear boundary for what counts as a polygon. It has to be a complete, unbroken loop of straight sides.

Polygon
A two-dimensional geometric figure with a finite number of straight sides that form a closed loop.

So, when you look at any shape, you can now analyze it like a geometer. Ignore the color, size, and orientation. Instead, ask yourself:

  1. Is it a closed figure?
  2. Are all its sides straight?
  3. How many sides does it have?
  4. How many vertices does it have?

Answering these questions will tell you exactly what the shape is, based on its defining attributes.

Quiz

Question 1:

Which of the following is a defining attribute of a triangle?

  1. It is pointing upwards.

  2. It is small in size.

  3. Its color is blue.

  4. It has three straight sides and three corners.

Question 2:

An artist draws a shape that has five straight sides, but leaves a small gap so the last side doesn't connect to the first. Can this shape be called a polygon?

  1. No, because polygons must be closed figures with no gaps.

  2. No, because polygons cannot have five sides.

  3. Yes, because the number of sides defines the shape.

  4. Yes, because it has straight sides.

Question 3:

The corners where the sides of a polygon meet are called its ______.

  1. vertices

  2. attributes

  3. points

  4. edges

Question 4:

If you change a defining attribute of a shape, like adding an extra side to a square, the shape itself changes.

  1. True

  2. False

Question 5:

Imagine a large, green square. Which of the following is a NON-defining attribute?

  1. The fact that it has four vertices.

  2. The fact that it is large.

  3. The fact that it has four sides.

  4. The fact that it is a closed figure.
